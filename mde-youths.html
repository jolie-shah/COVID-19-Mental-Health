Prevalence of Youths with Major Depressive Episodes

  1. Home
  2. The Statistics

COVID-19 is not only a deadly pandemic that has caused the entire world to shut down, but it is also a catalyst for severe psychological distress to adults and children alike. The symptoms, casulaties, and fear of the deadly virus along with the social isolation and disruptions to our daily lives brought by the lockdowns has amplified the growing mental health crisis. I hypothesized that regions with severe COVID-19 patterns would correspond to the prevalence of mental illnesses in youth. In this analysis, I compared the relationship between the regions with the most COVID-19 cases per 100,000 and the prevalence of youth with at least major depressive episode (MDE) in those regions from 2019-2021.

I collected data from the top 10 U.S. states with the most COVID-19 cases per 100,000 of all time as of August 6, 2021 and compared it to the prevalence of major depressive episodes (MDE) in youths. Below is a ranking of the two lists side-by-side. The underlined text are states that are among the top 10 of both lists.

Figure 1

Figure 1 is an interactive line graph displaying the prevalence of youths with MDE's of the top 10 U.S. states with greatest prevalence increase between 2019 to 2021. The states in blue (Arizona, Oklahoma, Arkansas, Utah, South Carolina, North Dakota) are states that both in this list and also in the top 10 states with the highest COVID-19 cases per 100,000. They were also underlined in the lists above. The states in green are states that are solely in this list of the greatest increase of youth MDE's.

Method: Figure 1 was created by statistics from Mental Health America's The State of Mental Health In America. These reports document national data in areas such as mental health in adults, mental health in youths, prevalence data, and access to care from 2015 to 2021. In this graph, I took information from the "Statistical Data For Youth With At Least One Major Depressive Episode (MDE) In the Past Year" section, which takes into account of both mental illness and psychological distress from a six year time frame. When analyzing the data, I took the rates of all U.S. states in 2019 and subtracted them from the rates in 2021 and organized them by the highest difference in change to lowest. The top 10 states are shown on Figure 1 with their prevalence of youths with MDE's from 2015-2019. The line graph gives a great visualization on the severe cases of MDE in U.S. states.

Analysis: It is interesting to see that exactly half of the states in the top 10 highest increase in prevalence of youths with MDE's are also in the top 10 most COVID-19 cases per 100,000. In the graph, there is an overall trend. From 2015 to 2017, rates steadily rise. Then, from 2017 to 2018, rates mainly stablize with a slight increase. From 2019 to 2021, rates skyrockets with the highest change in New Mexico at a 4.82 percent increase. The average increase in prevalence of these 10 states is 3.10 percent.

FIGURE 2: States with the Most COVID-19 cases per 100,000

Figure 2

Figure 2 is a graph displaying the top 10 U.S. states with the most COVID-19 cases per 100,000 and their prevalence of youths with MDE's over the past six years. The states in blue are identical to the blue states in Figure 1 and are five states that are within both top 10 lists. The states in red (Rhode Island, South Dakota, Tennesse, Florida) are states that are solely in the top 10 states with the most COVID-19 cases per 100,000.

Method: In the New York Time's Coronavirus in the U.S.: Latest Map and Case Count, there is a State Trends section, where I looked at the states with the most cases per 100,000 of all time. The 10 states at the top of the list were used to create Figure 2. Like Figure 1, it graphs the prevalence of youths with MDE's in each state using statistics from Mental Health America's The State of Mental Health In America. The distinction between the two figures are the states I selected. While Figure 1 displays the states with the highest increase in the prevalence of youths with MDE's, Figure 2 only presents the states with the most COVID-19 cases per 100,000.

Analysis: The states in blue are among the highest prevalence of youths with MDE's in the country, while the states in red are not. Despite this, all of these red states have shown an increase except Rhode Island, which showed a 0.32 percent decrease. The average increase in prevalence of these 10 states is 2.01 percent.



Conclusion:

The general structure of both Figure 1 and Figure 2 is a distinct rise of the prevalence of youths with at least one major depressive episodes. Even before the pandemic, states has already been showing an escalating trend. Now, with the pandemic amplifying this mental health crisis, the rates has gone up higher than ever. Five of the ten states with most COVID-19 cases per 100,000 has also been experiencing a more signifcant increase than the rest of the country. Over 1.5 billion student's education has radically altered due to COVID-19, and the world is rushing to return to normalcy after dealing with the social challenges that the pandemic brought along with it.
